UX & Visual Interaction at Code for America

Connecting small businesses in Puerto Rico with the incentives for economic growth.

During my Code for America Fellowship I worked with the government of Puerto Rico to address their huge budget deficit in economic development. We conducted over 80 hours of user interviews in Puerto Rico and spent several weeks combing this research and defining our user and MVP. We found that there was a surplus of government incentive programs for small businesses that the small business owners didn’t know about.

PrimerPeso is an application that guides business owners through 10 questions to receive filtered results of incentive programs they are eligible for. I designed the user experience and was responsible for the look and feel of our product including how we communicated our progress to our partners and stakeholders.

In Puerto Rico, many citizens had had unpleasant interactions with government websites so I had the challenge of presenting them with something that looked official and trustworthy but was beautiful and pleasant to use.

My main concern was that we developed something that served the small business owner and solved their problems of searching for funding. My second concern was that they had a pleasant experience on the website. Most citizens’ connotation of the design of government websites was very bad, so it was important to use non-government-y colors and illustrate financial concepts in a delightful way.

In order to pull this off I fully owned the CSS (LESS) and style of the application. I worked closely with my developer teammate to learn the information architecture of a node.js application and execute the front-end styling of our application. I also learned to read and write Jade.

I sharpened my visual design skills as well as my coding skills. I did a lot of illustration to communicate the types of incentives they would find on the site. I loved translating abstract concepts into something beautiful.

One of the most rewarding parts of developing this product was conducting user testing. Validating (or not!) our assumptions along the way made our product strong from the very beginning and kept my design priorities clear and intentional.

In user testing - through the use of different colors, custom illustrated icons, and intuitive user interactions - the user’s attitude completely changed. They forgot their built-up biases and enjoyed clicking through and receiving easy-to-read information. They were empowered and confident about making the decision to apply for these incentives.

By opening up this knowledge and designing a great user experience, small business owners are better equipped to find the resources they need to grow their own operations as well as fueling the larger economy.

Having so much responsibility on this project was incredible, and exactly what I wanted from my Code for America Fellowship experience. I was able to sketch something, mock it up, make the illustrations, and then code the real thing and have it up and running within a week.

Tools I used on this application were: Lots of notebooks and sketchbooks, Sketch, Webflow for prototyping, Illustrator for graphics, Atom text editor for coding, LESS, and Node.js

We took great care to document our process along the way. You can read more in our team blog here and check out our year-end report.
